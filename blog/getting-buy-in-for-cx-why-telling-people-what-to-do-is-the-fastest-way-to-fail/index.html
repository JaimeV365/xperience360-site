Getting Buy-In for CX: Why Telling People What to Do Is the Fastest Way to Fail

June 20264 min readBy Jaime Valle
Blog#CX#customer experience#CX programme#change management#stakeholder management#buy-in#cross-functional collaboration#customer journeys#journey mapping#internal alignment#silos#customer personas#Segmentor

Introduction

Find out why so many CX programmes struggle to gain traction within organisations, and how to transform internal resistance into genuine collaboration. This article explores a practical, human-centred approach to securing real buy-in from colleagues across departments without triggering defensiveness.

The Defensive Wall Most CX Teams Hit

I recently sat down with a senior leader from a well-known global institution, and the conversation quickly turned to one of the most common — and frustrating — challenges in customer experience work: persuading your colleagues to support CX initiatives.

You know the pattern: you present data, highlight pain points and propose changes, and suddenly the room goes cold. People cross their arms. They feel criticised. They start to feel like an outsider is telling them how to do their job. Understandably, this doesn't go down well.

The harder you push, the more resistance you create. Defensiveness kicks in, silos strengthen and your well-intentioned customer experience (CX) programme quietly stalls.

The Secret Isn’t Telling — It’s Inviting

The shift that changes everything is this: stop telling people what to do. Instead, invite them to join the conversation early on so that they can help shape the solution.

Most organisations face the same underlying issues: siloed teams and data, friction between departments, unclear workflows, customers being asked the same questions repeatedly, and painful case handovers. These aren’t just CX problems — they affect everyone.

CX teams have a unique advantage here. Rather than positioning ourselves as critics or external consultants, we can act as facilitators working in the interests of the customer (and, by extension, the employees who serve them).

Break Silos by Bringing Everyone to the Table

Organise workshops that focus on real customer personas and end-to-end journeys. Invite everyone who interacts with customers, including sales, account managers, the onboarding team, customer support, finance, and even legal and compliance.

The more, the merrier!

Work together to answer practical questions such as:

  • How can we stop asking customers the same thing twice?
  • How can we design customer journeys for our different personas or cohorts?
  • How can we ensure information flows smoothly across systems instead of getting stuck in silos?
  • Etcetera

When people co-create the journey map and are involved in analysing the problem and the solution, they stop seeing it as “someone else’s criticism” and start seeing it as an improvement to their own process.

Build a CX Programme That Benefits Everyone

Once the conversation is open, invite everyone to contribute: customer-facing teams, employees on the ground and the relevant departments, such as legal, finance and operations, to provide input on practical constraints.

Together, design a CX programme strategy that works for the whole organisation. Appoint programme champions in every department. Ensure that the customer is properly represented in decision-making processes.

Then monitor not just complaints, but also behaviours, community participation, event feedback and engagement signals. This early detection of risks and opportunities benefits everyone:

  • Support and CS teams can improve first-call resolution and handle queries more effectively.
  • Sales and account managers will know exactly who to approach, and when, regarding upgrades or renewals.
  • Compliance, legal and finance teams have clearer expectations and can optimise their time and outcomes.
  • Marketing and community teams can monitor social mentions, reviews and online sentiment in real time, converting feedback into stronger customer relationships and generating new leads.

Tools like Segmentor.app are incredibly powerful in this respect. By combining loyalty metrics with behavioural signals, you can gain a deep understanding of different customer (and employee) segments — and precisely know what to do next with each group to strengthen their relationship with your brand and encourage positive word-of-mouth.

The Real ROI of Buy-In

Ultimately, the key to achieving genuine buy-in is straightforward: demonstrate clear ROI for everyone involved.

When colleagues see how eliminating customer friction can make their work easier and lighter, and help them to look good personally, they will be more willing to support the change. People support what they help create, especially when it improves their daily lives.

Conclusion: Facilitate, Don’t Dictate

Rarely does CX success inside an organisation come from pushing harder. It comes from inviting the right people to the table early on, facilitating honest conversations and developing solutions together that benefit the customer.

The next time you sense defensiveness, pause. Instead of presenting the answer, ask, 'What would this look like if we designed it together?'

You may be surprised at how quickly silence can turn into collaboration, and at how much stronger your CX programme can become as a result.
