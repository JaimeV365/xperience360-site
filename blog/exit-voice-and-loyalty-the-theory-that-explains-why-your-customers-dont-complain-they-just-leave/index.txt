<p class="mb-6">I recently had a fascinating conversation with my colleague <a href="https://substack.com/@businessclown" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Andrea Montuschi</a>, who is a true authority in the field of Employee Experience (EX).</p>
<p class="mb-6">At one point, he mentioned that he often refers to Albert O. Hirschman’s <em>Exit, Voice, and Loyalty</em><sup>1</sup> when reviewing employee experience programmes with clients.
This immediately caught my attention.</p>
<h3 id="what-is-this-theory-actually-about">What is this theory actually about?</h3>
<p class="mb-6">As the curious (and rather impulsive) individual that I am, I purchased the book the same day. Then I started to read it, and I have to say that the text is not the most accessible, easy-to-read airport novel, but rather dense academic prose.</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">Yet beneath the scholarly prose lies a remarkably powerful idea that resonated instantly with me. Hirschman asks a basic question:</p>
<blockquote>
<p class="mb-6">What do people do when things start going wrong?</p>
</blockquote>
<p class="mb-6">His answer:</p>
<ul>
<li>They leave (exit)</li>
<li>They complain (voice), or</li>
<li>They stay, depending on how loyal they feel</li>
</ul>
<p class="mb-6">That’s the model. Three concepts. No fancy diagrams. No scoring system. No dashboards.</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">Just behaviour.</p>
<h3 id="the-uncomfortable-truth-it-reveals">The uncomfortable truth it reveals</h3>
<p class="mb-6">Hirschman talks about 'exit', but what’s really interesting is what happens beforehand. Or, more precisely, what doesn’t happen.</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">Because a large group of customers never complain. You might expect them to raise issues or give feedback, but many simply don't — and this can be especially true among younger generations, who may be less inclined to complain directly. They don't provide negative feedback or escalate issues. They just disappear.</p>
<p class="mb-6">Silent defectors.</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">No signal. No warning. No second chance.</p>
<p class="mb-6">Many organisations still operate under the implicit assumption that: 'If customers or employees are unhappy, they will let us know.' However, Hirschman challenged this idea long before the Millennials and Gen Zers came along. His central insight is that people do not always voice dissatisfaction. Often, they simply leave.</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">The critical nuance is that:</p>
<ul>
<li>When 'exit' is easy, 'voice' tends to diminish.</li>
<li>When 'exit' is difficult, 'voice' becomes more likely.</li>
</ul>
<p class="mb-6">In other words, exit and voice function as substitutes. If you make departure effortless, you may inadvertently silence one of your most valuable sources of feedback.</p>
<h3 id="why-this-should-concern-every-cx-and-ex-professional">Why this should concern every CX and EX professional</h3>
<p class="mb-6">If you work in customer or employee experience, this observation should make you think twice. It highlights a significant blind spot in the way we usually measure sentiment.</p>
<ul>
<li>Surveys capture voice.</li>
<li>Dashboards track feedback volume.</li>
<li>Reports applaud a decline in complaints.</li>
</ul>
<p class="mb-6">Yet customers may be leaving quietly. Employees may resign without explanation. And this silence goes unnoticed.</p>
<p class="mb-6"><strong>This is where Segmentor fits perfectly.</strong>
As I worked through Hirschman’s framework, one connection became strikingly clear: Hirschman explains behaviour.
<a href="https://segmentor.app/about" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Segmentor</a>, on the other hand, explains the consequences.</p>
<h3 id="loyalty-is-the-most-complex-element">Loyalty is the most complex element.</h3>
<p class="mb-6">According to Hirschman's model, it is loyalty that encourages people to choose 'voice' over immediate 'exit'.
In theory, this is a good thing. In reality, however, loyalty can manifest in various ways:</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">Genuine emotional attachment; routines and habits; absence of viable alternatives; and high switching costs. etc.Importantly, however, loyalty does not guarantee constructive behaviour. Loyal customers or employees do not necessarily become advocates. Sometimes, they can become deeply committed critics.</p>
<p class="mb-6">If, like me, you are an enthusiast of the <a href="https://xperience-360.com/blog/revisiting-the-apostles-model-understanding-customer-behaviour-in-todays-context/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Apostles Model</a>, you will probably see a lot of commonalities here and will now be thinking about 'hostage' customers who fit this same idea of being loyal but not too happy.</p>
<h3 id="bringing-the-two-frameworks-together">Bringing the two frameworks together</h3>
<p class="mb-6">For me, Hirschman’s model is not valuable for its framework itself. It lies in the tensions it highlights:</p>
<ul>
<li>Exit vs. voice</li>
<li>Silence vs. signal</li>
<li>Loyalty vs. reality.</li>
</ul>
<p class="mb-6">Hirschman provides the underlying mechanism: why people choose to leave or speak up. <a href="https://segmentor.app/about" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Segmentor</a> provides the necessary segmentation: who is speaking, how they are speaking, and what their departure truly signifies.</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">Together, they offer a far more complete picture.The essential question shifts from 'Are we receiving feedback?' to 'Who is providing feedback, through what channel, and what is the silent majority actually doing?'</p>
<h3 id="a-provocative-reflection">A provocative reflection</h3>
<p class="mb-6">If you make it easier for customers to leave than to voice their dissatisfaction, you may succeed in reducing visible noise and improving certain metrics.
However, you are not improving the Customer Experience.
You are merely rendering dissatisfaction invisible.</p>
<h3 id="final-thoughts">Final thoughts</h3>
<p class="mb-6">If your organisation celebrates low complaint volumes, you should take a closer look, because you might not have solved anything. You may simply have made it easier for people to leave quietly.</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">Hirschman’s model is often presented as an elegant, compact framework. However, its true value lies in the uncomfortable realisation it prompts:
Silence does not necessarily mean satisfaction.
If you only listen to those who choose to speak, you risk missing the many who have already walked away.</p>

<p class="mb-6">If you make it easier for customers to leave than to voice their dissatisfaction, you may succeed in reducing visible noise and improving certain metrics.
However, you are not improving the Customer Experience.
You are merely rendering dissatisfaction invisible.</p>
<h3 id="final-thoughts">Final thoughts</h3>
<p class="mb-6">If your organisation celebrates low complaint volumes, you should take a closer look, because you might not have solved anything. You may simply have made it easier for people to leave quietly.</p><p class="mt-6 mb-6">Hirschman’s model is often presented as an elegant, compact framework. However, its true value lies in the uncomfortable realisation it prompts:
Silence does not necessarily mean satisfaction.
If you only listen to those who choose to speak, you risk missing the many who have already walked away.</p>
